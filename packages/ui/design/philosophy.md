# Design Philosophy

Japanese aesthetic principles that form the foundation of all design decisions. These are not stylistic preferences—they are structural frameworks for determining what belongs in an interface and what should be omitted.

## Ma (間) — Interval

The space between things is structural. It creates rhythm, adjacency, and meaning. Ask what an interval is doing before filling it.

## Kanso (簡素) — Simplicity Through Omission

Subtract to clarify. Every element that doesn't directly serve function or clarity weakens the whole. This is not minimalism as aesthetic; it is removing what blocks direct experience.

Question every button, label, and border. If it does not earn its place, remove it.

## Wabi-sabi (侘寂) — Imperfection and Time

Beauty exists in the incomplete and the aged. Design for how systems evolve and degrade, not how they look on day one. Prefer honest representation over artificial polish.

## Shibui (渋い) — Understated Depth

Highest quality doesn't announce itself. It rewards sustained attention. Shibui is restraint + hidden craft detail—a surface that reveals more the longer you look.

Reserve visual intensity for critical interactions. Quality is measured by experiential effect per element, not by element count.

## Zen Aesthetics — Emptiness as Orientation

Form is contingent on purpose. The goal is not minimalism as a look, but a stance: non-attachment to decoration, direct experience, attentional clarity. Design becomes a tool for focus, not a subject of attention.

The interface should disappear when used. User content, not the container, is what matters.

## Decision test

- Decide what your negative space is doing before you fill it.
- Test for sterile emptiness: if reduction removed resonance, you subtracted the wrong things.
- Prefer honest materials and representation.
- Understatement should reward attention, not obscure function.
